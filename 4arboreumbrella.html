§ 02 / Design & CAD Modelling

SolidWorks · 14 components · full assembly

The design process began with street-level observation in London — broken umbrellas discarded after minor damage revealed a clear pattern: ribs and stretchers fail first, rendering an otherwise intact product worthless. The brief focused on modularity as a structural principle, not merely an aesthetic one.

The rib count was deliberately reduced from 12 to 6, enabling a larger cross-section per rib — a 2.4 × 5 mm rectangular profile in 6061-T6 aluminium — improving second moment of area and reducing stress concentration at the tip-shaft connector. This also simplified mechanism geometry at both tip and runner assemblies.

The end tip assembly uses a spring-pin quick-release: an inner pin with two sprung-fit joints locks into side holes on the tip-shaft connector. Pressing both pins simultaneously allows the tip to slide free, releasing the canopy holder and ribs in under 90 seconds without tools — the core IP of the product.

The runner assembly mirrors this at the lower connection. Six circular holes accept the spherical ends of stretchers; a snap-fit stopper locks them via sprung joints. The handle uses an identical pin-release mechanism, enabling handle swaps for customisation.

§ 03 / Material Selection

Weighted scoring · mechanical performance × sustainability

For the structural frame, anodised 6061-T6 aluminium emerged as the optimal choice: excellent strength-to-weight ratio (yield 241 MPa at 2,700 kg/m³), 10× better corrosion resistance than steel when anodised, and full recyclability. Using a single alloy throughout eliminates bimetallic corrosion at joints — a primary failure mode in conventional umbrellas.

The rPET canopy achieves the highest weighted score at 1.95 due to its closed-loop recycling potential, inherent water resistance without PFAS coatings, and low embodied carbon of 0.45 kg CO₂e/kg — 74% below virgin polyester. Stainless steel grades 303, 314, and 301 spring steel were selected for all pins, fasteners, and springs, providing fatigue resistance for 10,000+ open/close cycles.

  • Carbon fibre & fibreglass — low recyclability score, eliminated despite mechanical advantages. Does not meet the Arboré sustainability mandate.
  • 7075 Al — higher cost with marginal strength gain over 6061. Bimetallic corrosion risk at mixed joints. Rejected.
  • Titanium — excellent properties but eliminated on cost (£24.80/kg vs £1.25/kg for 6061) and processing complexity.
  • Ebony wood — CITES restrictions and deforestation risk; eliminated on sustainability grounds. Ipe selected for handle instead.

§ 04 / Structural Analysis

SimScale FEA · 18 m/s wind load · cantilever bending

Wind loading was calculated using Bernoulli's equation: at 18 m/s the dynamic pressure P = ½ρv² yields 198.5 Pa acting across the projected canopy area of 1.163 m², producing a total lateral wind force of approximately 231 N. This was distributed across 6 ribs, giving a per-rib loading of 38.5 N — the design-critical case for the 2.4 × 5 mm rectangular rib profile.

Cantilever bending analysis at the tip-shaft connector showed that the maximum bending stress of 89 MPa sits well below the 6061-T6 yield strength of 241 MPa, yielding a structural safety factor of 2.71. Tensile loading on the spring-pins in the quick-release tip assembly was verified against AISI 303 stainless steel (UTS 620 MPa) under repeated open/close cycles.

SimScale FEA validated the rib geometry under peak storm conditions: maximum von Mises stress 92 MPa (SF = 2.62), maximum rib deflection 18.3 mm at the tip — within serviceability limits. The 6-rib geometry with enlarged cross-section outperforms the conventional 12-rib design in stress distribution while reducing total part count by 50%.

§ 05 / Sustainability & LCA

Cradle-to-gate · 4.78 kg CO₂e/unit · 42% reduction

The Arboré umbrella achieves a cradle-to-gate footprint of 4.78 kg CO₂e per unit — a 42% reduction against the industry benchmark of 8.20 kg CO₂e. The dominant contributor is the aluminium shaft assembly (2.91 kg CO₂e), reflecting aluminium's energy-intensive production. However, the anodised 6061 alloy eliminates the corrosion-driven replacement cycles that dominate the conventional umbrella's long-run environmental cost.

The rPET canopy contributes only 0.077 kg CO₂e — 74% below an equivalent virgin polyester canopy — at an embodied factor of 0.45 kg CO₂e/kg. Process savings are equally significant: laser cutting eliminates 30% material waste, and RF welding replaces PFAS-based waterproof spray, saving 0.25 kg CO₂e per unit in avoided chemistry.

The most meaningful metric is time-adjusted: where a conventional umbrella produces 7.03 kg CO₂e per year of use over its 14-month lifespan, Arboré produces only 0.48 kg CO₂e per year over 10 years — a 93% reduction in annualised impact.

§ 06 / Commercial Plan

3-year projections · £300k seed · DTC model

Arboré enters the market at £72 per unit — positioned 30% below premium competitors (Hedgehog at £95–£120) while offering the only fully modular repair system in the segment. The pricing reflects a 63% gross margin on the umbrella unit, with recurring revenue through seasonal canopy releases (£18/canopy) and a subscription model at £60–£80/year.

A seed round of £300,000 carries the business through the first 18 months: 40% to prototyping and inventory, 30% to marketing and pre-orders, 20% to brand and e-commerce, and 10% to legal and contingency. The direct-to-consumer model via the Arboré website, supported by DPD circular logistics for repair and return, keeps distribution lean through Year 1.

Revenue is projected to grow from £443k in 2025 to over £7M by 2027, with consistent gross margins above 40% and break-even at month 18. By Year 3, EBITDA margin reaches 33% and ROCE 95%, supporting a potential 6× return on the initial seed investment.
