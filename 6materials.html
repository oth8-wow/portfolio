The UK's total primary energy production fell from its peak of 288.7 Mtoe in 2000 to just 106.6 Mtoe in 2021 — the lowest figure since 1970. The primary driver was the deliberate wind-down of fossil fuel extraction: coal collapsed almost entirely, while primary oil and natural gas each fell by nearly 70% from their respective peaks.

The COVID-19 pandemic created a sharp but temporary contraction in 2020 — transport energy dropped 28%, services by 8.4% — before rebounding in 2021–22 with the re-opening of workplaces, schools, and international aviation. The geopolitical shock of the Russia–Ukraine conflict disproportionately affected mainland Europe; for the UK, security risk was limited but price exposure was severe.

  • Forties Pipeline shutdown 2021 — 3-week maintenance outage drove a 21% production uptick in Q2 2022, totalling 27.4 Mtoe.
  • Primary electricity & bioenergy — the only two categories growing since 1990; bioenergy rose from 0.7 → 12.8 Mtoe over 31 years.
  • Nuclear forecast — 9 active reactors, 6.5 GW current; new-build programme targets 24 GW by 2050 (25% of UK electricity).
  • 1.1 million renewable installations by end 2021 — greatest since 2015, adding 7,200 MW of PV-led capacity.

Thermoelectric Generators

UCL EMS522U · 2022 Each technology surveyed faces the same core tension: the materials that make it work best — platinum, bismuth alloys, neodymium — are either scarce, expensive, or extracted through environmentally inconsiderate processes. Sustainability cannot be achieved by technology alone; it requires a materials rethink at every layer.
